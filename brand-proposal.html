Building India's First
Prebiotic Sugar Replacer Brand

Complete brand proposal — naming candidates, packaging design, identity system, and the full ₹10Cr investment plan. From Inspire Labs to Revelations Biotech.

Prepared by Inspire Labs For Ashok Reddy, CEO — Revelations Biotech Date May 2026
01 · Context
The Opportunity
Revelations Biotech manufactures sc-FOS — a prebiotic fiber that tastes like sugar, feeds gut bacteria, and costs 28.6% less than the nearest branded competitor. No consumer brand exists for it in India. This document covers four decisions needed before anything else moves: what to name the brand, what the packaging looks like, what the identity system is, and the full ₹10Cr investment to build it.

02 · Brand Naming
10 Name Candidates
Each name was evaluated against six criteria: memorability (can someone recall it after hearing once?), meaning and relevance (does it evoke sweetness, health, or natural origin?), bilingual fluency (does it work in Hindi and English, can a Tier 2 shopkeeper say it?), domain availability (.com/.in), trademark clearance risk (India Class 30 + Class 5), and shelf presence (will it stand out next to Sugar Free?). Scores are out of 10.

Tier 1 — Strongest Candidates

These three names scored highest across all six criteria. Any of them can carry a ₹500Cr brand.

Recommend

AURAA

/aw-RAA/ · ऑरा
From Latin "aurum" (gold) + the double-A gives it an Indian cadence (like Tara, Arya). The name literally means gold — matching the brand's entire visual DNA.

Why it works: AURAA is the only name where the name, the color system, the packaging, and the cultural meaning all point to the same thing: gold. In Indian culture, gold is the most auspicious element — Lakshmi, weddings, Dhanteras. On a hexagonal gold jar with a bamboo cap, "AURAA" in Playfair Display foil-stamped in gold on cream reads as a ₹1,000 product sold at ₹249. It is premium without pretension. A mom in Banjara Hills says "pass me the Auraa" as naturally as a gym-goer in Koramangala says "I switched to Auraa." The word exists in English ("aura" = radiance, energy field), making it globally portable for export. Two syllables. Five letters. One dominant vowel sound. Easy to spell after hearing once.

Tagline pairing: "AURAA — Sweetness That Glows." / "AURAA — Your Daily Gold."

Hindi resonance: "ऑरा" sounds natural in Hindi. "Yeh Auraa daal do chai mein" (Put some Auraa in the tea) — flows effortlessly. The double-A ending mirrors Indian name patterns (Amara, Tara, Sitara).

Shelf test: Imagine the sweetener aisle — green Sugar Free, blue Equal, brown stevia packets. Then a gold hexagonal jar with "AURAA" in warm serif type. It doesn't compete with them; it exists in a different universe.

NOOR

/noor/ · नूर
Urdu/Hindi/Arabic for "light, radiance, divine glow." One of the most beautiful words in the Hindustani language. Universally understood across India.

Why it works: NOOR carries deep emotional resonance across Hindu, Muslim, Sikh, and secular Indian audiences — it transcends religion and means pure radiance. As a sweetener brand, it says "this lights up your food and your health." The word is already in everyday Indian vocabulary ("uska noor hi alag hai"), so zero education cost on pronunciation. Four letters. One syllable. Impossible to misspell. On gold packaging, NOOR reads like a jewelry brand that happened to make food — which is exactly the premium positioning this product needs. The sound is warm, round, and inviting — the phonetic opposite of clinical names like "Sugar Free" or "Equal."

Tagline pairing: "NOOR — Light In Every Spoon." / "NOOR — The Glow of Good Sugar."

Risk: "Noor" is a common first name and may have some trademark congestion. Needs thorough Class 30 search. Multiple brands use "Noor" in cosmetics/beauty but fewer in food. ".com" likely unavailable; "noorsweetener.in" or "getnoor.in" are viable alternatives.

SOLRA

/SOHL-rah/ · सोलरा
Evokes "sol" (sun, gold, warmth in Latin/Spanish) + "-ra" suffix common in Indian brand names. A completely invented word with built-in warmth.

Why it works: SOLRA sounds like it has always existed. It has the warmth of "solar" without being literally solar. The -ra ending is extremely natural in Indian languages (Meera, Sitara, Amara), making it feel indigenous even though it is invented. As a blank-canvas name, it carries zero baggage — no existing brand associations to fight. Five letters. Two syllables. The "Sol-" beginning triggers subliminal associations with sun, gold, warmth, and energy — exactly what you want for a golden prebiotic sweetener. In Playfair Display on a gold jar, "SOLRA" has visual weight and elegance. It sounds like a ₹2,000Cr company.

Tagline pairing: "SOLRA — Sweetness From the Sun." / "SOLRA — Naturally Radiant."

Trademark advantage: As a coined word, SOLRA has the strongest trademark defensibility of any name on this list. No prior use in Indian food categories. .com availability strong for coined words.

Lifestyle Play

BARE

/bair/ · बेयर
English: minimal, nothing to hide, transparent, stripped-down truth. The clean-label D2C ethos in four letters.

Why it works: BARE is the anti-Sugar Free. Where Sugar Free hides behind chemical names and clinical packaging, BARE says "we have nothing to hide — look at our single ingredient." It taps the same transparent-label movement that built The Whole Truth into a ₹216Cr brand. Four letters. One syllable. Impossible to forget. The word works in Hindi conversation ("woh BARE sweetener use karti hai"). On a minimalist gold jar with just the word "BARE" and nothing else, it is devastatingly simple. The risk: "Bare" could feel cold or empty to some Indian consumers who associate food with warmth and abundance.

Tagline pairing: "BARE — Just FOS. Nothing Else." / "BARE — The Honest Sweetener."

GOLD DROP

/gohld drop/ · गोल्ड ड्रॉप
Literal and visual: a golden drop of sweetness. Describes what you see (golden powder/liquid) and what you do (drop it in your chai).

Why it works: GOLD DROP is what Paper Boat is for beverages — a name that paints a picture. You can see it: a golden drop landing in a cup of chai. It is descriptive enough for instant category recognition ("Oh, it's a sweetener") and distinctive enough to own shelf space. Tier 3 — Bold Directions

These names take bigger creative swings. Higher risk, higher reward.

Challenger

KANAKA

/kah-NAH-kah/ · कनक
Sanskrit/Hindi: gold, wheat, radiance. In South Indian languages, Kanaka directly means gold. The Kanakadasa connection adds heritage weight.

Why it works: KANAKA is unapologetically Indian and unapologetically premium. While other health brands chase Western-sounding names, KANAKA claims deep Sanskrit heritage — "gold" in its oldest form. Three syllables is longer than ideal, but the rhythmic ka-na-ka has a musical quality that makes it memorable. In Karnataka and Andhra Pradesh (Hyderabad is home base), "Kanaka" is immediately understood as gold. The name doubles as a subtle nod to Kanakadasa — the 16th century poet-saint — giving it cultural depth that resonates with educated Indian consumers.

Risk: Three syllables. May feel too Sanskritic for younger, metro consumers. Regional recognition is strong in South India but weaker in North.

ROOT

/root/ · रूट
Where FOS literally comes from — chicory root. Also: the root of good health, getting to the root of the problem (sugar), rooted in nature.

Why it works: ROOT is the ingredient-as-brand play. Like "Monk Fruit" or "Oat Milk" — the name educates and differentiates in a single word. "Where does your sweetener come from?" "A root." 03 · Brand Identity
Design System
The consumer brand identity is distinct from the Inspire Labs corporate identity. Inspire Labs uses Hanken Grotesk, Ink (#0E0F0A), and orange (#FF7A45) as active-state accent. The consumer sweetener brand uses a warm gold palette designed for shelf impact and food-category trust. Both systems share the principle of restraint — no decorative clutter, every element earns its space.
