Section 01

Design Philosophy

The foundational beliefs that shape every visual decision — how we want the brand to feel, not just look.

Creative Vision Statement

"We design for the moment a mother feels confident in what she feeds her family. Our visual language is the warmth of a golden morning in an Indian kitchen — science you can feel in your bones, not just read on a label. We are the antidote to clinical health branding: human, earthy, radiant, and deeply rooted in care."

Five Design Principles

01

Warm Over Clinical

Every design decision should evoke warmth, not sterility. We use golden light where competitors use fluorescent white. We choose handcrafted textures over lab-clean surfaces. Our science is conveyed through beautiful data, not intimidating jargon. Where Sugar Free feels like a pharmacy, we feel like a kitchen. Where stevia brands feel like a health store, we feel like home. Current design trends in health food branding (2025-2026) strongly favor authenticity and warmth over clinical minimalism — brands that embrace natural imperfection and emotional resonance are outperforming sterile competitors.

02

Premium but Accessible

We look like a brand that costs more than it does. Gold and cream signal quality, but rounded forms and friendly typography keep us approachable. We never intimidate with luxury — instead, we elevate the everyday. A sachet of our sweetener should feel like a small daily luxury, not a medical prescription or an exclusive indulgence. Research in packaging color psychology confirms that gold tones signal classic quality, tradition, and understated elegance — particularly effective when paired with warm, natural materials. The visual language balances aspiration with familiarity, premiumisation linked to storytelling and cultural proposition.

03

Science Made Beautiful

FOS prebiotics are backed by genuine science — gut health, blood sugar management, calcium absorption. But we never present this as cold data. Scientific information is rendered through elegant infographics, warm illustrations, and digestible visual stories. Our clinical evidence is presented with the same care as a beautifully plated dish: precise, considered, and inviting. The anti-AI crafting trend in 2026 design favors deliberate warmth and handmade quality over machine-perfect polish — even our data visualizations should feel human-touched.

04

Indian-Modern Fusion

We draw from India’s rich visual heritage — Mughal geometric patterns, textile weaving motifs, rangoli symmetry, brass utensil warmth — but render them in a contemporary global design language. This is not nostalgic pastiche; it is living tradition. A paisley becomes an abstract gut-health diagram. A rangoli dot pattern becomes our texture system. Brass tones inform our gold palette. The result is unmistakably Indian but never dated. Indian FMCG design best practices emphasize clear visual hierarchy and high-contrast color combinations for rapid identification in tightly packed retail aisles — our cultural elements must enhance shelf presence, not compete with legibility.

05

Trust Through Consistency

Health brands live or die by trust. Our visual system is rigorous and consistent — every touchpoint, from a sachet to a billboard to an Instagram story, feels unmistakably like the same brand. This consistency itself communicates competence, reliability, and care. We have rules not because we are rigid, but because our customers deserve to recognize and trust us instantly, every time. Studies show 93% of consumers make purchasing decisions based on visual appearance, including color, which impacts their perception of quality and trustworthiness.

Visual Mood Board

Textures

Linen cloth in cream and ecru. Raw wood grain — teak and mango wood. Hammered brass surfaces. Handmade paper with visible fiber. Terracotta with a soft matte finish. Jute and cotton weave. Honey dripping on warm stone. FOS powder dissolving in golden liquid.

Environments

A sunlit Indian kitchen in the early morning. A grandmother’s brass container shelf. A modern apartment with traditional touches. A farm with chicory root plants swaying in wind. A clean laboratory with warm wood accents. A family dining table set for chai time.

Colors & Lighting

Golden hour light streaming through a window. Warm amber tones with deep shadows. Cream and gold interplay. Touches of sage green from fresh herbs and leaves. Natural daylight, never fluorescent. Candlelight warmth. The amber glow of jaggery and honey. 2025-2026 food photography trends confirm that soft, directional natural lighting dominates — side lighting for texture, backlighting for glow and dimension.

Materials

Glass jars with brass lids. Matte-finish packaging with embossed elements. Recycled kraft paper. Satin-touch labels. Cotton drawstring pouches. Wooden measuring spoons. Ceramic bowls in earth tones.

Anti-Mood Board

What We Are NOT

These are explicit visual territories we actively avoid — not because they are bad design, but because they belong to competitors or communicate values misaligned with our brand.

  • Clinical White: The sterile, hospital-like white backgrounds used by Sugar Free and pharmaceutical health brands. We are food, not medicine.
  • Neon Green Health: The oversaturated, “look how natural I am” green that dominates stevia brands. We use sage, not lime. Sophistication, not desperation.
  • Instagram-Perfect Sterile: The overly styled, zero-imperfection flat-lay aesthetic. Our food has crumbs, our chai has steam, our kitchens have character. 2026 photography trends confirm: perfect plating is no longer the goal — realness is.
  • Supplement Packaging: Black bottles with silver text, aggressive typography, and fitness-bro energy. We are for families, not bodybuilders.
  • Cartoon Mascots: Animated characters, talking spoons, dancing sugar cubes. Our warmth comes from authentic human moments, not manufactured cuteness.
  • Tech-Health Futurism: Blue gradients, circuit-board patterns, DNA helixes floating in space. Our science is grounded, tangible, and warm.
  • Discount FMCG: Cluttered labels, starburst “NEW!” badges, yellow price tags, 20 fonts on one panel. We communicate value through design quality, not visual noise.
  • Western Wellness Minimalism: Bare white packaging with a single leaf, vaguely Scandinavian. We have cultural richness and are not afraid to show it.

Design Heritage: Indian Influences, Modernized

Our visual identity draws from five streams of Indian design tradition, each translated into contemporary application:

Mughal Geometry

The precise, interlocking geometric patterns of Mughal architecture — seen in jali screens and inlay work — inform our pattern system. These are abstracted into clean, repeatable motifs used in packaging accent bands and digital backgrounds. The mathematical precision communicates trustworthiness; the organic curves within the geometry communicate warmth.

Textile Traditions

Block-printing rhythms from Rajasthan, ikat weaving patterns from Telangana (our home state), and Banarasi brocade gold work all influence our texture choices. The warp-and-weft metaphor aligns beautifully with prebiotic fiber — literally and figuratively woven into the food you eat. Gold thread on cream fabric is essentially our primary color palette.

Rangoli Symmetry

The radial symmetry of rangoli — created fresh each morning as an act of care — mirrors our daily-use positioning. Dot-grid patterns from kolam and rangoli become our subtle background texture. The ephemeral, nurturing nature of rangoli perfectly embodies the Caregiver archetype: beauty created in service of others.

Brass & Copper Ware

The warm metallic tones of traditional Indian brassware — the dabba, the lota, the thali — directly inform our gold palette. These objects are simultaneously everyday and beautiful, functional and heirloom. Our gold is specifically calibrated to this brass warmth, not Western jewelry gold or cold metallic finishes.

Botanical Illustration

The Company School paintings and Mughal miniature botanical studies — detailed, scientifically accurate yet artistically beautiful renderings of Indian flora — inspire our illustration style. The chicory root, the sugarcane stalk, and the ingredients in our customers’ recipes are all rendered with this dual scientific-artistic sensibility.

Calligraphic Warmth

Urdu nastaliq and Devanagari calligraphy both use flowing, connected curves that communicate warmth and humanity. While our typography is primarily Latin-script, the organic flow and thick-thin contrast of Playfair Display echoes this calligraphic warmth. Multilingual packaging will honor each script’s native beauty rather than forcing all text into a single grid.

Section 02

Logo System — Five Concept Directions

Five distinct creative directions for the brand mark. Each is presented as a fully realized concept with construction logic, typography, and application guidance. The final name is flexible — these concepts adapt to any chosen name.

Concept 1: The Bloom

A seed unfurling into a five-petal flower — prebiotics nurturing growth from within

The Bloom concept uses a stylized five-petal flower form, where each petal represents one of the five core prebiotic benefits: gut health, immunity, bone strength, blood sugar management, and weight wellness. The petals emerge from a central seed form, conveying the idea of something small (a spoonful of sweetener) nurturing expansive well-being. The overlapping petal structure creates depth and warmth, moving from opaque gold at the front to translucent amber at the back. This concept resonates with the 2025-2026 health food design trend toward organic, non-geometric forms that feel alive and growing.

Typography

Wordmark set in a custom-modified rounded geometric sans-serif based on Outfit or Nunito Sans. The letterforms have softened corners (2px radius on all terminals) and slightly widened counters for an open, friendly feeling. The ‘o’ characters are perfectly circular, echoing the flower’s central seed. Letter-spacing is optically adjusted at +10 units for breathing room. The typeface contrast with the organic icon creates a “nature meets precision” balance that is core to our Sage+Caregiver archetype blend.

Icon Construction

Built on a 64×64 grid with 4px outer padding. The five petals are ellipses (14×28 unit proportion) rotated at 72-degree intervals from center. Each petal overlaps the center by 30% of its length. The central circle is 20% of the total icon diameter. A secondary seed dot sits at dead center at 10% diameter. All curves are mathematically derived, not hand-drawn — ensuring perfect reproduction at any size. The golden ratio (1.618) governs the petal-to-center proportion for inherent visual harmony.

Color Application

  • Primary: Gold gradient (135deg, #D4A017 → #F59E0B) on cream or white background
  • Reverse: Cream (#FFF8E7) on deep charcoal (#1a1d27)
  • Monochrome: Single-color gold or charcoal for embossing and single-color print
  • Metallic: Gold foil stamp for premium packaging applications
  • Seasonal: Festive variants with Diwali gold shimmer or Holi color splash (limited use, pre-approved only)

Size Variants

  • Primary: Icon + Wordmark horizontal (min 120px wide digital, 35mm print)
  • Secondary: Icon + Wordmark stacked (for square spaces)
  • Icon Only: Flower mark alone (min 32px digital, 10mm print)
  • Favicon: Simplified single-layer flower at 16×16px, center seed only
  • App Icon: Flower on cream background, 1024×1024 master, with rounded corners per platform

Best Used For

Consumer-facing packaging, retail signage, social media, brand advertising. This is the most emotionally resonant concept — ideal if the brand leads with heart and warmth over clinical credibility. Strongest alignment with the Caregiver archetype. Recommended as primary direction for launch.

Concept 2: The Spoon

A measuring spoon elegantly integrated into the lettermark — food-first, friendly, functional

The Spoon concept integrates a measuring spoon silhouette into the wordmark itself, creating a logomark that is inherently about food and daily use. The bowl of the spoon can be formed by the counter of the letter ‘P’ (if the brand name starts with P) or ‘b’, with the handle extending as a natural stroke of the letterform. This approach is warm, immediately communicative (this is something you eat with), and differentiates sharply from the pill-and-capsule visual language of supplement brands. Aligns with the tactile, sensory design trend dominating 2026 — objects you can almost feel through the packaging.

Typography

A warm serif-sans hybrid wordmark. The initial letter (containing the spoon) is set in a medium-weight serif (Playfair Display-inspired) while the remaining letters use a complementary rounded sans-serif (Inter or Nunito). This creates a natural visual hierarchy where the spoon-letter anchors the mark. The serif carries warmth and tradition; the sans-serif adds modern readability. At small sizes, the spoon detail simplifies to a single curved stroke within the letter counter.

Icon Construction

The spoon is constructed from two elements: an ellipse (the bowl) with a 3:2 width-to-height ratio, and a tapered line (the handle) extending at a 35-degree angle. The ellipse is positioned to align with the counter space of the letter. The overall icon sits on a 48×48 grid with 4px padding. The spoon bowl’s interior has a subtle concavity suggested by a secondary inner ellipse at 60% size and 30% opacity, giving the 2D form a sense of depth without true 3D rendering.

Color Application

  • Primary: Spoon in gold (#D4A017), wordmark in charcoal (#1a1d27) on cream background
  • Reverse: Spoon in gold, wordmark in cream on charcoal
  • Single-color: Entire mark in gold, charcoal, or cream depending on substrate
  • Embossed: Spoon element debossed on packaging for tactile premium feel

Size Variants

  • Primary: Full wordmark with integrated spoon (min 140px wide)
  • Secondary: Standalone spoon icon extracted from letterform
  • Favicon: Spoon bowl as simple ellipse at 16px
  • App Icon: Spoon on cream, centered, 1024×1024

Best Used For

Packaging, recipe content, food-focused marketing, sachet design. This concept excels on small formats where the spoon immediately communicates “food product.” Strongest for D2C e-commerce where the product needs to be instantly understood in a thumbnail. Also powerful for recipe content and social media where the spoon motif creates recognizable branding across formats.

Concept 3: The Helix

A fiber/DNA double helix — science-forward, modern, trust-building through visible intelligence

The Helix concept uses a stylized double-helix form that references both the molecular structure of FOS (fructo-oligosaccharide chains) and the broader metaphor of biological health. The two intertwining strands can represent the dual benefit of sweetness + prebiotic nutrition, or the connection between gut health and overall wellness. Nodes along the helix can represent specific health benefits. This is the most science-credible direction — positioning the brand as genuinely innovative, not just another sweetener with a health claim.

Typography

A geometric sans-serif wordmark — Space Grotesk or Outfit at Medium weight. The letterforms are precise, with consistent stroke widths and open apertures. The helix motif can be subtly woven through the wordmark by connecting the top of one letter to the bottom of the next with a fine curved stroke. Letter-spacing is tight (-20 units) for a confident, modern feel. This is the most “startup-tech” of the five typeface approaches, balancing scientific credibility with approachability.

Icon Construction

Built on a 64×64 grid. Two sinusoidal curves interweave, crossing at exactly 3 points within the frame. Each curve has a stroke weight of 3px (scaling proportionally). Four circular nodes (4px radius) sit at the crossing points and endpoints. The overall shape fits within a vertical rectangle (roughly 2:3 ratio). The helix is oriented vertically by default but can rotate to horizontal for inline applications. The gradient from gold to sage along the path represents the journey from food to health.

Color Application

  • Primary: Gradient from gold (#D4A017) to sage (#7CB342) along the helix path
  • Single-color: Full gold on dark, full charcoal on light
  • Scientific context: Gold helix on deep charcoal with white wordmark for maximum credibility
  • Animated: Nodes pulse in sequence along the helix (see Motion section)

Size Variants

  • Primary: Helix icon left + wordmark right (min 150px wide)
  • Secondary: Helix stacked above wordmark
  • Icon Only: Helix mark (min 28px — complex detail requires slightly larger minimum)
  • Favicon: Simplified to two crossing arcs at 16px

Best Used For

B2B communications, clinical/scientific marketing materials, healthcare professional outreach, investor decks, conference branding. This concept builds maximum credibility with medical professionals and health-conscious consumers who research ingredients. Ideal for the “Prebion Labs” umbrella identity if the brand architecture separates consumer and scientific sub-brands.

Concept 4: The Circle

A continuous cycle representing the gut health journey — wholeness, completeness, perpetual care

The Circle concept uses a continuous circular form to represent the ongoing cycle of gut health: consume, nourish, thrive, repeat. The circle is one of the most universally trusted shapes — it communicates completeness, protection, and unity. Different segments of the circle can be color-coded to represent different aspects of the prebiotic benefit cycle (intake, digestion, absorption, benefit). The brand’s initial letter sits at the center, grounding the abstract form in brand identity. An outer ring of dashed marks suggests the microbiome ecosystem surrounding the core promise.

Typography

The wordmark uses a classic serif — Playfair Display at SemiBold — set either inside the circle (for the stacked version) or beside it (for the horizontal version). When inside, the text follows a slight curve along the lower arc. When beside, it sits on a straight baseline with generous tracking (+30 units). The contrast between the geometric circle and the warm serif creates a “modern tradition” tension that is core to the brand. Sub-brand text (“Labs” or “Prebiotic Sweetener”) in Inter Light, all caps, tracked at +200 units.

Icon Construction

Primary circle: 60% of total mark width. Outer accent ring: 76% of total width, dashed (8px dash, 4px gap). The circle is divided into segments by color, though the stroke is continuous (no breaks). A center circle holds the initial letter at 26% of the total width. The construction is based on concentric circles on a 64×64 grid. The golden ratio (1.618) defines the relationship between inner and outer ring diameters for inherent visual harmony.

Color Application

  • Primary: Multi-segment circle (gold, amber, sage, cream) with gold center on charcoal background
  • Simplified: Single-color gold circle for small applications
  • On packaging: Circle as a seal/badge element, embossed or foil-stamped
  • Animated: Circle draws itself clockwise, segments lighting up in sequence

Size Variants

  • Primary: Circle + horizontal wordmark (min 130px wide)
  • Secondary: Circle with wordmark inside/below
  • Seal: Circle used as a quality seal or badge on packaging
  • Favicon: Simplified circle with initial at 16px — no outer ring

Best Used For

Trust-building contexts: packaging seals, certification badges, official communications, partnership lock-ups. The circle inherently feels “approved” and “complete,” making it excellent for building consumer confidence. Also strong for app icons where circular forms are native to both iOS and Android.

Concept 5: The Monogram

A bold, overlapping lettermark — premium, minimal, luxury-health positioning

The Monogram concept reduces the brand to its essential letters — a bold “P” or overlapping “PB” monogram (adaptable to final name initials). This is the most premium direction, drawing from luxury fashion’s approach to identity. The letters overlap with varying opacity, creating depth. A fine underline or frame element adds structure. This concept bets that the brand will become recognizable enough that initials alone carry meaning — a long-term investment in brand equity that pays dividends as awareness grows.

Typography

Playfair Display Bold is the base typeface for the monogram letters, chosen for its high stroke contrast (thick-thin variation) which creates visual interest even in just one or two characters. The serifs are refined, not heavy — bracketed with a slight calligraphic influence. For the full wordmark version, the name is set in Playfair Display SemiBold with +40 unit tracking and an all-caps treatment for the sub-line (e.g., “LABS” in Inter Light, tracked at +200 units beneath).

Icon Construction

The monogram sits on a 64×64 grid within a square frame (60×60 active area, 2px padding). The two letters overlap by 25-30% of their individual widths. The front letter is at 100% opacity, the rear at 35-40%, creating a layered dimensional effect. A 1.5px horizontal rule sits below the baseline at exactly the descender line. Optional: a fine square frame (1px stroke, 4px corner radius) contains the monogram for formal applications. The overlapping creates a negative-space effect that rewards closer inspection.

Color Application

  • Primary: Gold letters on charcoal, or charcoal letters on cream — always high contrast
  • Luxury print: Gold foil monogram on matte black or deep navy stock
  • Digital: Gold monogram with subtle gradient on dark backgrounds
  • Single-color: Works beautifully in any single brand color

Size Variants

  • Primary: Monogram + full wordmark below (for formal use)
  • Secondary: Monogram alone (for social media profile pics, app icons)
  • Favicon: Single initial “P” at 16px — the most legible of all five concepts at tiny sizes
  • Oversized: Building signage, trade show backdrops — the monogram scales up dramatically

Best Used For

Premium positioning, brand extensions, Prebion Labs umbrella identity, gift packaging, corporate communications, investor materials. This concept has the longest brand-building runway and the highest ceiling for perceived brand value. It requires more marketing investment to build recognition but pays dividends in brand equity over time.

Section 03

Logo Usage Rules

Protecting the integrity of the brand mark across all applications. These rules apply regardless of which concept direction is chosen.

Clear Space

The clear space around the logo is defined as 1.5 times the x-height of the lowercase ‘b’ in the wordmark (or 1.5 times the icon height if using icon-only). No text, imagery, borders, or other graphic elements may intrude into this zone. This ensures the logo always breathes and commands visual authority regardless of layout density.

Clear Space Specifications

  • Horizontal lockup: 1.5x on all four sides measured from outermost elements
  • Stacked lockup: 1.5x on top and sides, 1.0x between icon and wordmark, 1.5x below
  • Icon only: 1.5x icon height on all sides
  • In confined spaces: Minimum 1.0x if 1.5x is physically impossible, with brand manager approval

Co-Branding Lock-Up Rules

When the brand logo appears alongside partner, certification, or endorsement logos:

  • Partner logo always to the left; brand logo to the right (reading order = partner introduces brand)
  • A 1.5px vertical divider line in 30% opacity gold separates the two logos
  • Brand logo may be 10-20% larger than partner logo to maintain visual dominance
  • Clear space rules still apply — minimum 1.5x b-height between divider and each logo
  • If more than two logos, arrange left-to-right by hierarchy with dividers between each
  • On small formats (sachet), co-branding is not permitted — only the brand logo appears
  • Certification marks (FSSAI, organic, vegan) are placed separately in regulatory zone, not in co-brand lock-up

Color Misuse — 10 Things to Never Do

  1. Never use gold text on white backgrounds for body text — fails WCAG AA. Reserve gold for large headlines (24px+) or icons only on light backgrounds. Use charcoal for all readable text on light surfaces.
  2. Never combine sage green and amber side by side at full saturation — creates visual vibration. Always separate with a neutral (cream, charcoal, or white space).
  3. Never use tertiary line-extension colors for core brand — Berry, Ocean, and Sunrise are reserved exclusively for their respective product lines. Core brand uses only primary and secondary palettes.
  4. Never apply gradients to body text — gradients are for backgrounds, icons, and decorative elements only. Text must always be a solid color.
  5. Never add opacity to gold below 60% on dark backgrounds — faded gold on dark looks muddy. Use a gold tint (lighter solid color) instead of transparency.
  1. Never use pure black (#000000) — always use Deep Charcoal (#1a1d27). Pure black creates harsh contrast that feels clinical, not warm.
  2. Never use neon or fluorescent greens — our green is sage (#7CB342), muted and sophisticated. Neon green signals “stevia brand” or “discount health store.”
  3. Never colorize photography with brand colors — photographs remain natural. Brand colors appear in overlays, text, and graphic elements layered on photos, never as tints on the photo itself.
  4. Never use more than 3 brand colors per composition — one primary + one secondary + one neutral is the maximum. More creates visual chaos undermining premium perception.
  5. Never match competitor color combinations — specifically avoid: green + white (Sugar Free), lime green + leaf (stevia brands), blue + white (pharma), red + yellow (mass FMCG).

Iconography

Icon Construction Rules

Grid System

  • Base grid: 24×24 pixels with 2px padding on all sides (20×20 live area)
  • Stroke weight: 1.5px consistently across all icons
  • Stroke caps: Rounded (never flat or square)
  • Stroke joins: Rounded (consistent with caps)
  • Corner radius: 2px minimum on all internal corners
  • Optical sizing: Circular icons extend to full grid; square icons inset 1px
  • Export: SVG (vector), PNG at 1x/2x/3x (raster), icon font (woff2)

Color Rules

  • On dark backgrounds: Gold (#D4A017) single-color
  • On light backgrounds: Charcoal (#1a1d27) single-color
  • Interactive hover: Amber (#F59E0B)
  • Disabled: 30% opacity of the applicable color
  • Never multicolor: Each icon is always a single color — never two-tone or gradient

Filled vs. Outlined

  • Outlined (default): Line icons for navigation, lists, general UI, packaging details
  • Filled: Active/selected states, packaging hero feature icons, social media graphics, large display use (40px+)
  • Transition: When an icon changes from outlined to filled (e.g., on selection), animate the fill with a 200ms ease-out transition

Illustration & Pattern System

Organic line art, botanical motifs, and culturally rooted patterns that bring the brand world to life beyond photography.

Illustration Style Guide

Technical Specifications

  • Line weight: 1.5–2px primary strokes, 0.75–1px detail strokes
  • Line color: Charcoal (#1a1d27) on light, Cream (#FFF8E7) on dark
  • Accent color: Gold (#D4A017) sparingly — max 20% of illustration area
  • Fill: Minimal — mostly outline with occasional flat fills in cream, blush, or sage at 20% opacity
  • Style: Organic curves, never perfectly geometric. Hand-drawn feel but digitally clean
  • Human figures: Warm, minimal, diverse. No photorealistic faces — suggest features with minimal marks. Diverse skin tones through warm browns and golds.
  • Cultural elements: Reference Mughal miniature botanical studies and Company School paintings for precision-with-beauty approach to plant/ingredient illustration

Five Core Illustration Concepts

  • 1. The Gut Health Journey: A flowing path from mouth to gut, with prebiotic fiber as golden threads weaving through the digestive system. Beneficial bacteria as friendly abstract circular forms. Used in educational content and packaging inserts.
  • 2. Ingredient Sourcing: Chicory root plants in field, close-up root structure. Sun, soil, water. Indian agricultural setting with subtle cultural markers. Used in “Our Story” and sourcing pages.
  • 3. Recipe Cooking: Hands adding sweetener to chai, steaming pot, scattered spices. Overhead kitchen view with Indian elements — brass vessels, steel glasses, ginger on board. Used in recipe cards.
  • 4. Family Moments: Multi-generational family at table. Grandmother pouring chai. Children in kitchen. Morning breakfast. Warm, inclusive. Used in lifestyle marketing.
  • 5. Morning Routine: Single figure in sunlit kitchen. Steam from cup. Jar on counter beside spices. Plants on windowsill. Calm, daily ritual. Used in social media and D2C onboarding.

Section 08

Photography Art Direction

Four photography styles that bring the brand to life — warm, authentic, and culturally rooted. Based on 2025-2026 food photography trends: natural lighting dominance, warm cozy aesthetics, authenticity over perfection, and props shifting toward ceramic, wood, and earth-toned palettes.

Product Photography

Hero Shots E-commerce Catalog

Lighting Setup

Key: Large softbox at 45° camera-left, slightly above, warm-gelled (CTO 1/4). Fill: Cream foam board camera-right, 2:1 ratio. Bounce: Gold reflector below and slightly behind for warm underlight. Background: Natural surfaces — teak wood, raw linen, honed stone — never infinity white.

Lens & Camera

85mm or 100mm macro. f/4-5.6 for context, f/8-11 for sharp catalog. Slight overhead angle (15-30°). Tripod-mounted. White balance: 5500-6000K (warm daylight).

Post-Processing

Shadows lifted 15-20%. Highlight roll-off gentle, never clipped. Saturation +5-10% warm tones only. LUT: “Golden Hour” warm film simulation. No HDR, no compositing.

Do’s & Don’ts

  • Do: Show product in use (spoon scooping powder, jar on kitchen shelf). Include natural props (cinnamon, cardamom, leaves).
  • Don’t: Photograph on pure white seamless. Don’t use artificial garnishes or non-food props. Don’t over-retouch the powder texture.

Lifestyle Photography

Brand Social Advertising

Lighting Setup

Primary: Natural window light, morning (7-10 AM) or late afternoon (4-6 PM). No artificial key lights. Fill: white reflector or sheer curtain diffusion. Practicals: visible warm light sources (kitchen pendant, stove flame).

Lens & Camera

35mm or 50mm prime. f/2.8-4 for natural bokeh. Handheld for authentic energy. Shoot in real Indian kitchens — not studios styled to look like homes.

Post-Processing

Film-like grain (ISO 400 equivalent). Desaturate blues/greens 10%, boost warm tones 10%. Skin tones warm, never orange. LUT: “Indian Summer” — warm, golden, slightly faded blacks.

Casting & Direction

  • Primary: Women 30-45, families, multi-generational households
  • Diverse skin tones reflecting India’s full range
  • Real expressions, natural conversation, unposed moments
  • Wardrobe: Contemporary Indian — cotton kurtas, casual sarees
  • Don’t: Overly affluent settings — middle-class authenticity

Food Photography

Recipe Social Content

Lighting Setup

Key: Overhead softbox or north-facing window for flat-lay. Side light for 45° angle. Backlighting: Essential for steam, liquid, glow. Warm-gelled. No flash — continuous or natural only.

Lens & Camera

50mm flat lay, 85mm close-up, 35mm table spread. f/2.8-4 hero dish, f/8 full spread. Overhead (90°) and 45° primary angles. Tethered for composition review.

Styling Direction

  • Authentic Indian food — chai poured, desserts served, dosa batter mixed
  • Sweetener naturally in scene, never forced
  • Props: brass plates, steel tumblers, wood boards, banana leaves, terracotta
  • Imperfection welcome: crumbs, drips, steam, scattered spices
  • LUT: “Kitchen Light” — warm, natural, minimal processing

Science Photography

Clinical B2B Education

Lighting Setup

Key: Clean, bright, warm-temperature. Large softbox. Accent: Warm-gelled rim light to avoid clinical coldness. Background: Matte cream or warm grey — never clinical blue or pure white. Wood elements at edges.

Lens & Camera

100mm macro for powder texture, dissolution, crystal structure. 50mm for lab context. f/2.8 shallow macro, f/11 sharp documentation. Tripod mandatory. Focus stacking for macro.

Subject Guidance

  • FOS powder close-up: crystalline texture, purity, white/off-white
  • Dissolution: sweetener in liquid (chai, water) — backlit for glow
  • Manufacturing: clean facility, QC, precision — warm-toned
  • LUT: “Warm Lab” — clean, bright, slightly warm shadows
  • Don’t: Stock lab photos with blue lighting. No cold clinical feels.
Section 09

Motion & Animation

Movement in the brand is organic, never mechanical — inspired by nature’s rhythms, not factory precision.

Motion Principles

Organic Easing

All motion uses custom cubic-bezier curves mimicking natural movement. Primary ease: cubic-bezier(0.25, 0.46, 0.45, 0.94) — gentle ease-out. Spring: cubic-bezier(0.34, 1.56, 0.64, 1) — slight overshoot for playful elements. Never use linear.

Duration Scale

Micro: 150-200ms (hovers, toggles). Standard: 300-400ms (reveals, transitions). Complex: 600-1000ms (hero reveals, logo). Maximum: 3s for any single animation. Entry 15% slower than exits — arriving is gentle, leaving is swift.

Stagger & Sequence

Multiple elements stagger 50-80ms between items. Maximum 6 in a stagger sequence; beyond that, use wave/cascade. Direction follows reading order: top-to-bottom, left-to-right. Never animate everything simultaneously.

Section 15

Visual Identity Compliance Checklist

30 verification points for any new creative asset. Every item must pass before approval.

Pre-Production Checklist

Logo & Brand Mark (5 points)

01
Correct logo version — primary, secondary, icon-only, or favicon as appropriate for format and size
02
Clear space respected — minimum 1.5x b-height on all sides, no elements intruding
03
Minimum size met — above thresholds for medium (35mm print, 120px digital for full logo)
04
No unauthorized modifications — no stretching, rotating, shadowing, outlining, recoloring
05
Correct background application — right version for light, dark, colored, or photographic backgrounds

Color (5 points)

06
Only approved brand colors — verified against hex/Pantone values in this document
07
Maximum 3 colors per composition — one primary + one secondary + one neutral
08
Accessibility contrast met — WCAG AA minimum for text, AAA for critical information
09
No color misuse violations — checked against 10 “Color Don’ts” list
10
Print: Pantone/CMYK verified — screen colors correctly converted for print production

Typography (5 points)

11
Correct typefaces — Playfair Display, Inter, JetBrains Mono, or Space Grotesk only
12
Type scale levels followed — all text maps to one of the 13 defined levels
13
Letter-spacing correct — negative for display, positive for overlines/labels
14
Maximum line width respected — body text never exceeds 70 characters per line
15
Hierarchy clear — visual distinction between heading levels, one clear entry point

Imagery & Illustration (5 points)

16
Photography style matched — warm lighting, natural surfaces, no clinical/cold aesthetics
17
No competitor visual cues — no green+white, no neon green, no blue clinical
18
Illustration style consistent — organic line art, 1.5px strokes, gold accents, minimal fills
19
Pattern usage correct — single pattern, 10-30% opacity, never on photography
20
Icons from approved set — brand icon library, correct stroke weight and single color

Layout & Composition (5 points)

21
Grid system followed — consistent margins, padding, and alignment
22
Visual breathing room — adequate negative space; not cluttered or dense
23
Premium perception — does it feel premium at first glance?
24
Brand warmth present — warm, approachable, caring (not cold or clinical)?
25
Co-branding rules followed — correct lock-up sizing and positioning if partners present

Regulatory & Final (5 points)

26
FSSAI license number — present on all food packaging and related materials
27
Veg/Non-veg symbol — mandatory green circle in green square on all food packaging
28
Nutritional info compliant — per FSSAI labeling, bold font for sugar/salt/sat-fat per RDA
29
Claims verified — all health claims approved by regulatory and backed by evidence
30
Brand manager sign-off — final proof reviewed and approved before production

How to Use This Checklist

Every new creative asset — social post, packaging revision, banner, or email — must be reviewed against all 30 points before production. The brand manager maintains a checklist log recording approval dates and exceptions.

Exception process: If a checklist item cannot be met due to format constraints, the brand manager may grant a documented exception with rationale. Exceptions are logged and reviewed quarterly.

Third-party partners: All external agencies, print vendors, and freelancers receive this Visual Identity System plus the Quick Reference Card. Brand assets distributed only through the approved Brand Assets folder.

Version control: This document is versioned. The current version (V2, May 2026) supersedes all previous visual guidelines. When this document is updated, all partners must be notified and provided with the new version within 5 business days.
