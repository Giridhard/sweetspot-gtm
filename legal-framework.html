SECTION 01

FSSAI Health Claim Regulations

Understanding what [Brand Name] can and cannot legally say about its FOS sweetener under India's food safety framework.

1.1 Governing Legislation

Health claims for food products in India are governed by the Food Safety and Standards (Advertising and Claims) Regulations, 2018, as amended. These regulations classify all claims a food business operator (FBO) can make into distinct categories, each with different approval requirements and evidentiary standards.

The regulations were notified on November 27, 2018, and have been amended multiple times since — most recently with the 2021 amendments that became final in 2022, and the May 2025 directive banning unsubstantiated "100%" claims.

1.2 Categories of Claims Under FSSAI

Category A: Nutrition Claims (Generally Permitted)

These are claims about the nutrient content of a food. They can be made without prior FSSAI approval provided they meet the criteria specified in Schedule I of the regulations. Examples include claims like "low sugar," "high fiber," "source of protein," etc.

Requirement: Must meet specific quantitative thresholds defined in the regulations. For example, to claim "source of fiber," the product must contain at least 3g of fiber per 100g of solid food.

Category B: General Health Claims (Permitted with Substantiation)

These describe a relationship between a food or its components and health. They do not require prior approval but must be scientifically substantiated with published, peer-reviewed research.

Examples: "Calcium is important for bone health" or "Dietary fiber contributes to digestive health." These are well-established nutrient-function relationships.

Requirement: Must be based on generally accepted scientific evidence. The FBO must maintain substantiation files that can be produced on demand during FSSAI inspections.

Category C: Reduction of Disease Risk Claims (Requires FSSAI Approval)

These claims suggest that consumption of a food or ingredient reduces the risk of developing a disease or health condition. They require prior approval from the Food Authority (FSSAI).

Examples: "FOS may reduce the risk of type 2 diabetes when consumed as part of a balanced diet" — this would require clinical trial data and formal FSSAI approval.

Requirement: Application to FSSAI with complete clinical trial evidence, systematic reviews, or meta-analyses. Approval timeline: 6–18 months. Cost: Significant (application fees + clinical study costs).

Category D: Therapeutic / Cure Claims (Absolutely Banned)

No food product may claim to prevent, alleviate, treat, or cure any disease, disorder, or physiological condition. This is an absolute prohibition under Regulation 4 of the Advertising and Claims Regulations.

Violation: These claims can result in penalties up to Rs.10 lakh per offense under Section 53 of the FSS Act, product recalls, and FSSAI license suspension or cancellation. In extreme cases, criminal prosecution is possible.

1.3 The FOS-Specific Challenge

Why FOS Claims Are Particularly Sensitive

Fructooligosaccharides (FOS) sit at a unique regulatory intersection. As a prebiotic fiber with documented health benefits in published scientific literature, the temptation to make strong health claims is significant. However, FSSAI treats FOS primarily as a food ingredient — not a nutraceutical or drug.

Critical distinction: If [Brand Name] positions FOS as merely a sweetener with nutritional benefits (fiber content, low glycemic index), it falls under standard food regulations. If it positions FOS as having specific prebiotic/therapeutic benefits (improving gut flora, reducing blood sugar), it risks crossing into nutraceutical or even drug territory, which triggers entirely different regulatory requirements under the FSSAI Nutraceuticals Regulations 2016 (amended 2022/2024).

Strategic recommendation: Position [Brand Name] as a "natural, low-calorie sweetener that is also a source of dietary fiber" — this keeps all claims within Category A (Nutrition Claims) territory, which requires no prior FSSAI approval.

1.4 Ten Compliant Claim Examples for [Brand Name]

Each claim below has been evaluated against the FSSAI Advertising and Claims Regulations. These are claims [Brand Name] can make on packaging, website, and advertising materials, provided the product formulation meets the specified nutrient thresholds.

COMPLIANT CLAIM #1: "Source of Dietary Fiber"

Category: Nutrition Claim (Schedule I)   PERMITTED

Requirement: Product must contain at least 3g of fiber per 100g (solids) or 1.5g per 100ml (liquids). FOS is approximately 95% dietary fiber by composition, so [Brand Name] easily exceeds this threshold.

How to use: Print on front-of-pack, use in advertising, include in product descriptions. Add an asterisk with "per serving" information on the nutrition panel.

COMPLIANT CLAIM #2: "Low Calorie Sweetener"

Category: Nutrition Claim   PERMITTED

Requirement: Product must contain no more than 40 kcal per 100g (solids) or 20 kcal per 100ml (liquids). FOS provides approximately 1.5–2 kcal per gram (vs. sugar's 4 kcal/g), so a typical serving would qualify.

How to use: Pair with calorie comparison to sugar. State exact calorie content per serving on the nutrition panel.

COMPLIANT CLAIM #3: "No Added Sugar"

Category: Non-Addition Claim   PERMITTED

Requirement: The product must not contain any added mono- or disaccharides or any food used for its sweetening properties. Since FOS is an oligosaccharide (not a mono/disaccharide), [Brand Name] qualifies for this claim if no sucrose, glucose, fructose, or other sugars are added.

Caution: Must include a statement "contains naturally occurring sugars" if the FOS raw material contains residual glucose/fructose/sucrose (typical FOS is 95% pure, with 5% residual sugars). Check your certificate of analysis from the FOS supplier.

COMPLIANT CLAIM #4: "Low Glycemic Index"

Category: Nutrition Claim   PERMITTED

Requirement: Must have clinical or laboratory evidence showing a GI below 55 (low GI threshold). FOS has a GI of approximately 0–10 according to published literature, making this an easily substantiated claim.

How to use: State the specific GI value with a citation. "Low Glycemic Index (GI <15)" is stronger and more credible than just "Low GI."

COMPLIANT CLAIM #5: "Suitable for People Managing Their Sugar Intake"

Category: General Health Claim   PERMITTED WITH SUBSTANTIATION

Requirement: Must be substantiated with evidence that the product can substitute sugar without significant caloric or glycemic impact. This is a lifestyle-directed claim, not a disease claim.

Caution: Do NOT say "suitable for diabetics" — that crosses into a disease-specific claim. "People managing their sugar intake" is a lifestyle choice, not a medical condition reference.

COMPLIANT CLAIM #6: "Natural Origin — Derived from Chicory Root / Sugarcane"

Category: Factual / Ingredient Origin Claim   PERMITTED

Requirement: Must be factually accurate. If FOS is derived from chicory root (inulin hydrolysis) or sugarcane (enzymatic synthesis from sucrose), state the actual source. Do not claim "natural" without qualification — FSSAI has cracked down on unsubstantiated "100% natural" claims as of May 2025.

Preferred wording: "Made from natural plant sources" or "Derived from chicory root fiber" rather than "100% Natural."

COMPLIANT CLAIM #7: "Prebiotic Fiber"

Category: Nutrient Function Claim   PERMITTED WITH SUBSTANTIATION

Requirement: FOS is recognized in published scientific literature as a prebiotic (selectively fermented by beneficial gut bacteria). The claim "contains prebiotic fiber" is a factual statement about the ingredient's composition. Maintain a substantiation file with 3–5 peer-reviewed references.

Caution: Do NOT extend this to "improves gut health" or "enhances immunity" without separate substantiation and potentially FSSAI approval. "Contains prebiotic fiber" is safe; "improves your gut microbiome" is not.

COMPLIANT CLAIM #8: "Tooth-Friendly — Does Not Contribute to Dental Caries"

Category: General Health Claim   PERMITTED WITH SUBSTANTIATION

Requirement: FOS is non-cariogenic (not fermented by oral bacteria to produce acids that cause tooth decay). This is well-established in dental literature. Maintain substantiation with published studies.

How to use: Particularly effective in marketing to parents and children's products. Can be supported by citing general scientific consensus on non-sugar sweeteners and dental health.

COMPLIANT CLAIM #9: "Half the Calories of Sugar"

Category: Comparative Nutrition Claim   PERMITTED

Requirement: The comparison must be factually accurate and must compare equivalent serving sizes. FOS provides approximately 1.5–2 kcal/g vs. sugar's 4 kcal/g. Claim must include specific numbers: "[Brand Name] provides approximately 2 kcal per gram compared to sugar's 4 kcal per gram."

Caution: Under FSSAI regulations, comparative claims must compare with the same category of foods. Comparing to sugar (a direct substitute) is permissible.

COMPLIANT CLAIM #10: "Zero Artificial Sweeteners — No Aspartame, No Sucralose, No Saccharin"

Category: Non-Addition / Negative Claim   PERMITTED

Requirement: Must be factually accurate — the product must genuinely contain none of these artificial sweeteners. This is a "free-from" claim that is permitted when the absence is meaningful to consumers.

Strategic value: Extremely powerful in India where consumer distrust of artificial sweeteners is high. Positions [Brand Name] as the "clean" alternative to aspartame-based and sucralose-based products.

1.5 Ten NON-Compliant Claim Examples (What NOT to Say)

Each of these claims would violate FSSAI regulations. Understanding why they are non-compliant is crucial for training your marketing team, briefing influencers, and reviewing advertising copy.

NON-COMPLIANT #1: "[Brand Name] Cures Diabetes"

Violation: Therapeutic/Cure Claim — Absolutely Banned   BANNED

Why: No food product can claim to cure, treat, or prevent any disease. Diabetes is a medical condition. This claim would violate Regulation 4 of the FSSAI Advertising and Claims Regulations AND Section 53 of the FSS Act, AND potentially the Drugs and Magic Remedies (Objectionable Advertisements) Act, 1954.

Penalty: Up to Rs.10 lakh fine, product recall, license suspension, and potential criminal prosecution under the DMR Act.

NON-COMPLIANT #2: "Clinically Proven to Lower Blood Sugar Levels"

Violation: Disease Risk Reduction Claim Without Approval   REQUIRES FSSAI APPROVAL

Why: Even if clinical studies exist showing FOS lowers blood glucose, making this claim without prior FSSAI approval is illegal. Blood sugar levels are directly associated with diabetes management, making this a disease risk reduction claim that requires Category C approval. The word "clinically proven" adds another layer of regulatory risk — it implies drug-like efficacy.

What to say instead: "Low glycemic index sweetener" (a factual nutrition claim that does not reference blood sugar reduction).

NON-COMPLIANT #3: "100% Natural and Pure"

Violation: Unsubstantiated Superlative Claim   BANNED SINCE MAY 2025

Why: FSSAI issued a specific directive in May 2025 banning the use of "100%" claims (including "100% pure," "100% natural," "100% safe") on food packaging, digital ads, and product literature. These terms have no scientific or regulatory definition and can mislead consumers. FOS undergoes enzymatic processing — calling it "100% natural" is scientifically indefensible.

What to say instead: "Derived from natural plant sources" or "Made from chicory root fiber."

NON-COMPLIANT #4: "Boosts Immunity and Prevents Infections"

Violation: Unapproved Health Claim + Disease Prevention Claim   BANNED

Why: "Boosts immunity" became a particularly scrutinized claim after the COVID-19 pandemic, with FSSAI cracking down on food products making immunity claims without substantiation. "Prevents infections" is a clear disease prevention claim that is absolutely prohibited for food products. Even if FOS has prebiotic properties that may indirectly support immune function, this claim goes far beyond what is permissible.

What to say instead: "Contains prebiotic fiber" (a factual ingredient statement).

NON-COMPLIANT #5: "Recommended by Doctors for Weight Loss"

Violation: Unauthorized Endorsement + Therapeutic Claim   BANNED

Why: Multiple violations — (1) Implying medical endorsement without specific, verifiable doctor recommendations. (2) Weight loss is considered a therapeutic outcome, not a nutritional benefit. (3) Under ASCI guidelines (updated April 2025), health/nutrition claims in advertisements must be backed by qualified professionals with disclosed credentials.

What to say instead: "Low calorie sweetener — contains approximately half the calories of sugar."

NON-COMPLIANT #6: "Better Than Sugar Free — Healthier Than Every Other Sweetener"

Violation: Disparaging Claim + Unsubstantiated Superlative   BANNED

Why: FSSAI regulations explicitly prohibit claims that undermine or disparage competitors' products. Naming a competitor brand directly is competitive disparagement. Additionally, "healthier than every other sweetener" is a superlative claim that cannot be substantiated — it would require comparative clinical evidence against every sweetener in the market.

What to say instead: "Unlike artificial sweeteners, [Brand Name] is derived from natural plant fiber." (This is a factual distinction, not a disparaging comparison.)

NON-COMPLIANT #7: "Helps You Lose 5 Kg in 30 Days"

Violation: Specific Quantified Health Outcome Claim   BANNED

Why: Promising specific, quantified health outcomes (weight loss amounts, blood sugar reductions, cholesterol numbers) is prohibited for food products. This crosses into drug/therapeutic territory. It also violates the Drugs and Magic Remedies Act if used in advertising. Even pharmaceutical weight loss drugs cannot make such definitive claims without clinical trial disclaimers.

What to say instead: "A lower-calorie alternative to sugar that can be part of a balanced diet and healthy lifestyle."

NON-COMPLIANT #8: "Safe for All Diabetics — No Blood Sugar Spike Guaranteed"

Violation: Disease-Specific Safety Claim + Absolute Guarantee   BANNED

Why: (1) "Safe for all diabetics" implies suitability for a specific medical condition, which requires FSSAI approval as a disease-related claim. (2) "No blood sugar spike guaranteed" is an absolute guarantee about a physiological outcome — no food product can make absolute physiological guarantees. Individual responses to food vary based on metabolism, medication, meal composition, and other factors.

What to say instead: "Low glycemic index sweetener — suitable for people who want to manage their sugar intake." (Lifestyle framing, not disease framing.)

NON-COMPLIANT #9: "Used in Hospitals Across India"

Violation: False/Unverifiable Institutional Endorsement   BANNED

Why: Unless [Brand Name] has documented procurement agreements with named hospitals, this claim is false and misleading. Even if some hospitals use FOS-based products, claiming "hospitals across India" implies widespread institutional medical endorsement. This violates both FSSAI advertising regulations and the Consumer Protection Act, 2019 (misleading advertisement provisions).

What to say instead: Only reference specific institutional partnerships if they exist and have given written permission for marketing use.

NON-COMPLIANT #10: "This Product Has No Side Effects"

Violation: Absolute Safety Claim   BANNED

Why: No food product can claim to have absolutely no side effects. FOS can cause gastrointestinal discomfort (bloating, gas, diarrhea) in some individuals, particularly when consumed in excess. Claiming "no side effects" is both scientifically inaccurate and legally dangerous — it creates product liability exposure if any consumer experiences an adverse reaction after relying on this claim.

What to say instead: "Generally well-tolerated. Excessive consumption may have a laxative effect." (This is the legally required warning for sugar alcohols and high-fiber sweeteners in many jurisdictions.)

1.6 FSSAI Claim Substantiation File — What to Maintain

FSSAI can demand evidence supporting any claim at any time during inspections. [Brand Name] must maintain a Claim Substantiation Dossier containing the following for each claim made:

  • Complete product specification sheet (composition, nutrient analysis per serving)
  • Certificate of Analysis (CoA) from the FOS raw material supplier showing purity, residual sugars, heavy metals, microbial limits
  • Laboratory test reports from FSSAI-accredited/NABL-accredited labs confirming nutrient content claims (fiber content, calorie content, GI value)
  • Published peer-reviewed scientific references supporting each health claim (minimum 3 studies per claim)
  • Systematic reviews or meta-analyses (preferred over individual studies)
  • Documentation of the dose-response relationship — at what serving size do the claimed benefits occur?
  • Any clinical studies conducted specifically on the [Brand Name] product (if available)
  • Copies of all advertising materials, packaging designs, website pages, and social media posts making claims
  • Internal review and sign-off records showing that claims were reviewed by a qualified food technologist before publication
  • Records of any FSSAI correspondence regarding claims, approvals, or queries

1.7 The May 2025 FSSAI "100% Claims" Ban — Impact on [Brand Name]

Critical Update: FSSAI Directive — May 2025

FSSAI has directed all food brands to immediately cease using "100%" claims on packaging, digital advertisements, and product literature. This includes "100% pure," "100% natural," "100% safe," "100% organic" (unless certified), and similar superlative formulations. Violations observed after the directive's issuance can result in immediate enforcement action.

Action for [Brand Name]: Audit ALL packaging, website copy, social media posts, and influencer scripts immediately. Replace any "100%" language with specific, substantiated claims. Flag this with your packaging printer — existing print runs with non-compliant language must be consumed or relabeled.

1.8 Claim Review Process — Internal SOP

Step-by-Step Claim Approval Workflow

  1. Marketing team drafts claim — any new claim for packaging, ads, social media, or influencer briefs
  2. Food technologist review — does the claim meet FSSAI regulatory thresholds? Is it substantiated?
  3. Legal review — does the claim violate any regulation? Is it phrased safely? Is there comparative or disparaging language?
  4. Substantiation file update — add supporting evidence to the dossier for the new claim
  5. Sign-off — written approval from food technologist + legal counsel before any claim goes live
  6. Post-publication monitoring — check that the claim appears as approved (no editorial changes by influencers, designers, or marketplace listing teams)

2.3 Comparison Advertising Rules

What ASCI Permits in Comparative Ads

  • Generic category comparison: "Unlike artificial sweeteners, [Brand Name] is derived from natural plant fiber" — PERMITTED (compares to a category, not a specific brand)
  • Factual nutrient comparison: "[Brand Name] contains dietary fiber; most artificial sweeteners contain zero fiber" — PERMITTED (verifiable factual claim)
  • Price comparison: "[Brand Name] costs Rs.X per serving, comparable to leading sweetener brands" — PERMITTED (factual, not disparaging)

What ASCI Prohibits in Comparative Ads

  • Named brand disparagement: Naming a competitor brand and making negative claims about it — BANNED
  • Cherry-picked comparisons: Comparing only on metrics where [Brand Name] wins while ignoring areas where competitors may be comparable — RESTRICTED
  • Misleading visual comparisons: Showing competitor products in negative light (dark colors, smaller portions, frowning consumers) — BANNED
Strategic Comparison Approach

Safest strategy: Compare [Brand Name] to sugar (not to competitor sweetener brands). "Replace sugar with [Brand Name]" is a category-expansion message that does not trigger comparative advertising scrutiny. Sugar is a commodity, not a branded product — you cannot "disparage" sugar. This also aligns with your positioning as a sugar replacement, not a sweetener-replacement.

3.2 Adverse Reaction Scenarios and Response Protocols

Critical: [Brand Name] Adverse Reaction Response Plan

FOS can cause gastrointestinal side effects (bloating, gas, cramping, diarrhea) in some individuals, particularly at doses above 20g/day. While these are generally mild and self-limiting, a consumer complaint about an adverse reaction must be handled with extreme care to limit legal exposure.

Scenario 1: Consumer Complaint of GI Discomfort

Risk Level: MODERATE

  1. Acknowledge immediately — customer service must respond within 24 hours with empathy and concern
  2. Document everything — date, product batch number, symptoms reported, quantity consumed, timeline of symptoms
  3. Do NOT admit liability — express concern, do not say "our product caused this." Say: "We take all consumer feedback seriously and want to understand your experience."
  4. Offer product replacement or refund — as a goodwill gesture, not as an admission of fault
  5. Retain the batch sample — if the consumer returns the product, retain it for testing
  6. Internal investigation — check the batch records, quality control test results, and any other complaints for the same batch
  7. Escalate if needed — if multiple complaints emerge from the same batch, initiate quality investigation and consider voluntary hold

Scenario 2: Allergic Reaction or Serious Health Event

Risk Level: HIGH

  1. Immediate medical recommendation — advise the consumer to seek medical attention immediately
  2. Notify legal counsel — within 4 hours of becoming aware of a serious adverse event
  3. Quarantine the batch — place an immediate hold on all products from the same production batch
  4. Notify FSSAI — under FSS Act, food business operators must report adverse events to the food authority
  5. Preserve all evidence — retain samples, production records, QC reports, raw material CoAs, and all communications
  6. Insurance notification — notify your product liability insurer within the policy-specified timeframe (typically 24–72 hours)
  7. Do NOT issue public statements without legal counsel review
  8. Independent lab testing — send batch samples to a NABL-accredited laboratory for analysis

3.5 Liability Limitation Strategies

Seven Strategies to Limit [Brand Name]'s Product Liability Exposure
  1. Proper Labeling: Include all mandatory warnings, allergen declarations, and usage instructions. "Excessive consumption may have a laxative effect" is critical for FOS products.
  2. Serving Size Recommendations: Clearly state recommended daily intake and maximum safe dose. For FOS, recommend 5–10g/day and warn against consuming more than 20g/day.
  3. Contractual Liability Transfer: Co-packer agreements must include indemnification for manufacturing defects and quality control failures.
  4. Product Liability Insurance: Maintain adequate coverage (minimum Rs.1 crore for a startup; increase to Rs.5 crore as sales scale).
  5. Quality Management System: Implement HACCP/ISO 22000. Documented quality systems demonstrate due diligence and reduce negligence claims.
  6. Terms of Service / Disclaimers: D2C website ToS should include liability limitation clauses (enforceable to a limited extent under Indian law, but useful as a defense layer).
  7. Rapid Response Protocol: A documented adverse event response plan demonstrates good faith and reduces punitive damages in litigation.
