Voice Architecture

The [Brand Name] Voice — Defined

Our voice is the soul of the brand expressed in words. It does not change across channels — only the tone adjusts. The [Brand Name] voice is that of a warm, knowledgeable friend who happens to be a food scientist and a caring parent. We speak with the confidence of evidence and the gentleness of someone who genuinely wants you to live well.

Think of a Hyderabadi nutritionist who trained at a top research university, runs a popular kitchen on Instagram, and always has time for your questions. That is our voice — rooted in science, expressed in warmth, seasoned with a hint of Indian character.

Archetype Expression

Primary: The Caregiver

The Caregiver archetype drives our core motivation: to protect and nurture. In practice, this means:

  • We always center the consumer's wellbeing over our sales goals
  • We speak about families, daily routines, and real-life moments
  • We use language that feels like a warm hand on the shoulder
  • We never create anxiety or fear to sell — we create comfort and confidence
  • We celebrate small, sustainable changes over dramatic transformations
Caregiver Voice Example
"Your morning chai is already a ritual you love. [Brand Name] just makes it one your gut loves too."

Secondary: The Sage

The Sage archetype fuels our credibility: to seek and share truth through knowledge. In practice, this means:

  • We cite research when making health claims — always
  • We educate before we sell — knowledge is the path to trust
  • We explain the "why" behind our product, not just the "what"
  • We welcome questions and never dodge the hard ones
  • We position ourselves as the brand that helps you understand, not just consume
Sage Voice Example
"FOS feeds the beneficial Bifidobacteria in your gut — think of it as a prebiotic fertilizer for the good bacteria that keep your digestion humming."

Understanding Voice vs. Tone

Voice is WHO we are. Tone is HOW we sound in a given moment. Our voice never changes — it is always caring, knowledgeable, approachable, and honest. But our tone shifts like a conversation: playful with friends on Instagram, precise with doctors in a clinic, warm with a new customer opening their first welcome email.

Below, we define the exact tonal settings for 20 distinct communication contexts. Each context includes three defining adjectives, a sample sentence, guidance on what to avoid, and the pillar dial settings (1-10) that shape the tone.

Hinglish Rules

Do

  • Use Hindi words when they are the most natural word in context (chai > tea, dahi > yogurt)
  • Use Hinglish for social media, WhatsApp, and casual email — channels where people actually talk this way
  • Romanize Hindi words consistently (always "meetha" not "meetha" / "mitha" / "mithaa")
  • Provide parenthetical English translation when needed for mixed audiences
  • Test Hinglish copy with native speakers — it must feel natural, not scripted
  • Use Hindi for emotional moments — pyaar, ghar, family celebrations

Do Not

  • Never force Hindi into English copy where it sounds awkward
  • Never use Hindi to appear "relatable" in professional contexts (investor, doctor, press)
  • Never mix Hindi grammar with English sentences ("Product very accha hai")
  • Never use Devanagari script on English-first channels — Romanize instead
  • Never use Hindi that requires translation for South Indian audiences without providing it
  • Never reduce Hindi to decorative tokenism — one random Hindi word in otherwise all-English content

Writing Style Guide

Grammar & Mechanics

Sentence Length

Average: 12-18 words. Maximum: 25 words. If a sentence exceeds 25 words, break it into two. Short sentences build pace and clarity. Long sentences lose the reader — especially on mobile, where 80%+ of our audience reads.

Good — 14 words
"[Brand Name] feeds the good bacteria in your gut with every spoonful."
Too Long — 38 words
"[Brand Name] is a prebiotic sweetener that, through its unique fructooligosaccharide composition, provides nourishment to the beneficial Bifidobacteria in your gastrointestinal tract, thereby supporting digestive health and immune function with every serving."

Paragraph Length

2-4 sentences per paragraph. On social media, one sentence per paragraph is acceptable. For blog content, never exceed 4 sentences before a line break. White space is our friend.

Active Voice (90%+ of all copy)

Active voice is clearer, shorter, and more confident. Passive voice is permitted only when the actor is unknown or intentionally de-emphasized.

Oxford Comma

Yes, always. In every list of three or more items, use the Oxford comma before "and" or "or." This prevents ambiguity and is the professional standard.

Correct
"[Brand Name] is great in chai, coffee, and dahi."
Wrong
"[Brand Name] is great in chai, coffee and dahi."

Numbers

  • Spell out one through nine. Use numerals for 10 and above.
  • Exception: Always use numerals for GI scores (GI 15), measurements (250g), prices (₹120), and percentages (73.4%).
  • Never start a sentence with a numeral — spell it out or restructure.
  • Use Indian numbering for large numbers: lakhs and crores for Indian audiences, millions for international.

Currency

  • Always ₹ symbol, no space between symbol and number: ₹120, not ₹ 120.
  • No decimals unless paisa are relevant: ₹120, not ₹120.00.
  • For international: USD $, AED, GBP £ — always with symbol.
  • Per-unit pricing: ₹0.48/g (note the slash, no spaces).

Capitalization

  • Sentence case for headlines, subheads, and body copy.
  • Title Case only for proper nouns: [Brand Name], Revelations Biotech, FOS.
  • Never ALL CAPS except for Amazon bullet lead-ins (per Amazon convention).
  • "Prebiotic" is lowercase unless starting a sentence.
  • "Gut health" is always lowercase.

Multilingual Voice Adaptation

Transcreation Principles

[Brand Name] does not translate content — we transcreate it. Translation preserves words. Transcreation preserves meaning, emotion, and cultural resonance. Every language adaptation must feel like it was written natively in that language, not translated from English.

Core Principles

  • Preserve warmth: The Caregiver tone must be felt in every language. If a translation sounds cold or clinical, it has failed.
  • Adapt references: "Morning chai" becomes "morning filter kaapi" in Tamil markets. Cultural specificity beats generic accuracy.
  • Respect idiom: Every language has its own way of talking about food, family, and health. Use those idioms, do not import English ones.
  • Test with natives: Every piece of translated content must be reviewed by a native speaker who is also a consumer — not just a translator.
  • Transcreation > Translation: Recreate the feeling, not the sentence. If a joke does not work in Telugu, write a new joke that does.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Literal translation of English idioms ("sweetness that loves you back" may not work literally in all languages)
  • Using formal/textbook register when colloquial is appropriate (and vice versa)
  • Ignoring regional food culture (do not mention dahi in markets where curd culture is different)
  • Using Google Translate or AI translation without human review
  • Applying Hindi creative to South Indian audiences without adaptation
  • Assuming one "Hindi" — Lucknowi Hindi, Bihari Hindi, Mumbai Hindi all sound different
